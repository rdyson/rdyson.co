I'm reading [The Power of Habit](https://www.amazon.com/Power-Habit-What-Life-Business-ebook/dp/B0055PGUYU) by Charles Duhigg. The big takeaways are: 1. Habits form in a particular part of our brains. Our brains optimize for making things automatic because that way we spend less energy thinking about "how do I ride a bike again?" Our brains look for certain triggers to set that automatic response in motion, so if you want to get into a habit, repeat the same steps over and over. Sure this might be what you've always heard, but I didn't know until now that there are significant neurological changes associated with building habits. 2. Habits never get 'unlearned', they just get changed. The part of your brain that is responsible for habits doesn't 'forget', so you have to either replace the response (for example, if you're trying to quit smoking the idea is to chew gum when your usual smoke-break cue fires) or to avoid the habit cues altogether. I'm oversimplifying, but this is the gist. The book has a lot of interesting anecdotes, about individuals and large corporations. If that's your sort of thing, I'd recommend it.
